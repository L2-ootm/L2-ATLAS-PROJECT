## 1. Market Entry Strategy: LatAm vs US/EU

### Recommended expansion sequence for a Brazilian fintech:

**Tier 1 — LatAm first (lower friction, shared regulatory DNA)**
- **Mexico**: Largest LatAm economy after Brazil, has CoDi/DiMo instant payment system (analogous to Pix), IFRS mandatory, LGPD-like federal privacy law (LFPDPPP), uses SAT tax system. Cultural proximity. Population ~130M.
- **Colombia**: President Petro has publicly urged expansion of Brazil's Pix to Colombia (2026). Strong fintech growth, has Transfiya instant payment system. IFRS mandatory. Uses IVA (VAT). Population ~52M.
- **Argentina**: Complex macro (currency controls, inflation), but large market. Uses RGCSRT for invoicing. Has Debin instant payment system. IFRS mandatory.

**Tier 2 — US (high reward, highest regulatory cost)**
- No federal sales tax — 45 states + DC each have their own rates, rules, exemptions. Some states tax groceries, some don't. Wayfair decision (2018) means economic nexus triggers obligations even without physical presence.
- US GAAP only (SEC does not permit IFRS for domestic listed companies). Separate accounting standard from IFRS.
- Money Transmitter Licenses required per state (50+ licenses). FinCEN MSB registration. CFPB oversight.
- No VAT — uses state-level sales tax, which is structurally very different from Brazilian ICMS/IPI/ISS.
- Payment infrastructure: ACH (slow, batch), FedNow (new instant rail launched 2023), RTP network. No equivalent to Pix.

**Tier 3 — EU/UK (mature, unified regulation, high compliance bar)**
- PSD2 requires strong customer authentication (SCA), open banking APIs.
- GDPR applies universally — data stays in EU, DPO required, 72-hour breach notification, fines up to 4% global turnover or €20M.
- UK Open Banking (CMA Order) goes beyond PSD2 with mandatory API standards.
- IFRS mandatory for listed companies in all EU member states.
- VAT system harmonized at EU level with minimum rates (standard must be ≥15%). Individual countries set standard rates: Germany 19%, France 20%, Spain 21%, Italy 22%, Netherlands 21%.
- SEPA payment rails (SCT, SCT Inst) — pan-European instant payments.
- 27 member states, single passport for financial services.

### Strategic recommendation:
Start with Mexico (largest LatAm opportunity, Pix-style system emerging, IFRS-native, cultural proximity). Then Colombia. Avoid US until LatAm revenue supports the massive compliance investment. EU/UK as parallel track via Ireland or Netherlands for passport access.

| **Argentina** | BCRA authorization | Banco Central de la República Argentina oversight. Payment institutions regulated. |

| Region | Framework | Key Requirements |
|---|---|---|

| **Mexico** | Open Finance | CNBV developing open finance framework. Not yet mandatory. |

## 4. Multi-Currency Architecture

### Design patterns for multi-currency cashflow platforms:

**Three-currency model:**
1. **Base currency** — the currency in which the entity maintains its books (e.g., BRL for Brazilian entity, USD for US entity, EUR for EU entity)
2. **Reporting currency** — the currency used for management/consolidated reporting (e.g., USD for global management, BRL for Brazilian parent reporting)
3. **Consolidation currency** — the currency used for group-level financial statements (typically the parent's functional currency or a stable reference like USD/EUR)

**Key architectural decisions:**
- Store all amounts with (value, currency_code, decimal_precision) — never assume precision
- FX rates: need multiple sources — central bank rates (BCB for BRL, ECB for EUR, Fed for USD), market rates for intraday, historical rates for reporting
- Transaction currency vs settlement currency: a BRL transaction settled via SWIFT in USD requires FX at settlement
- Unrealized vs realized FX gains/losses: IFRS requires separate disclosure
- Exchange rate date convention: transaction date rate vs period-end rate vs average rate (varies by IFRS/GAAP standards)

**IFRS requirements (IAS 21):**
- Functional currency determination based on primary economic environment
- Foreign currency transactions recorded at spot rate at transaction date
- Monetary items retranslated at closing rate
- Non-monetary items at historical rate
- Exchange differences recognized in profit or loss (or OCI for net investment hedges)

## 5. Cross-Border Payment Infrastructure

### Payment Rails by Type

**SWIFT:**
- 11,000+ financial institutions in 200+ countries
- ~50% of high-value cross-border payments use SWIFT (as of 2018)
- ISO 20022 migration underway (MX messages replacing MT messages)
- Three data centers: Netherlands, US, Switzerland — with European/Trans-Atlantic messaging zones
- Does NOT hold funds, does NOT perform clearing/settlement — purely messaging
- Settlement happens through correspondent banking or local RTGS systems

**Local Payment Rails:**

| Region | System | Speed | Coverage |
|---|---|---|---|
| Brazil | SPI (Pix) | Real-time (24/7) | All BCB-authorized institutions |
| Mexico | SPEI | Real-time | Mexican banking system |
| Colombia | CUD (Compensación Única Diaria) | T+1 | Main interbank system |
| US | ACH (batch), FedNow (instant), RTP (instant) | ACH: 1-2 days; FedNow/RTP: seconds | FedNow expanding; RTP covers ~70% of US deposits |
| EU | T2 (RTGS), SCT Inst (instant) | T2: same-day; SCT Inst: <10s | SEPA-wide |
| UK | CHAPS (RTGS), Faster Payments | CHAPS: same-day; FPS: seconds | UK-wide |

**Correspondent Banking:**
- Required for cross-border transfers where no direct relationship exists
- Each intermediary adds cost and latency
- Nostro/Vostro accounts: domestic bank holds foreign currency account at correspondent
- Trend: declining correspondent banking relationships (de-risking) — affects LatAm corridor access

**Fintech alternatives:**
- Wise (formerly TransferWise): uses local rails in 40+ countries, avoids SWIFT for retail
- Ripple/XRP: cross-border settlement (limited adoption)
- Stablecoins (USDC, USDT): emerging for cross-border B2B but regulatory uncertain

## 6. Multi-Entity Setup

### Legal Entity Structure by Region

**Recommended structure for a Brazilian fintech expanding internationally:**

1. **Parent entity**: Brazil (holding company or operating entity)
2. **LatAm subsidiaries**: Mexico (S.A. de C.V.), Colombia (S.A.S. or S.A.), Argentina (S.R.L. or S.A.)
3. **US**: Delaware C-Corp or LLC (most common for fintechs), with state-level MTL compliance
4. **EU**: Irish or Dutch BV/NV (for PSD2 passport access), or German GmbH (for EU market presence)
5. **UK**: Ltd company (post-Brexit, separate from EU)

### Transfer Pricing

- OECD Transfer Pricing Guidelines apply universally
- Brazil has specific transfer pricing rules ( Lei 14.596/2023 ) — now aligning with OECD guidelines
- Key: document intercompany transactions (management fees, IP licensing, cost-sharing)
- Arm's length principle: prices must match what unrelated parties would charge
- Mexico: strict transfer pricing documentation required (DIOT filing)
- EU: Country-by-Country Reporting (CbCR) for groups with >€750M revenue
- US: IRC Section 482, Form 5471/8865 for foreign entities

### Consolidated Reporting

- IFRS 10 requires consolidated financial statements for parent + subsidiaries
- Elimination of intercompany transactions
- Currency translation (IAS 21): translate foreign subsidiary financials to parent currency
- Minority interests (non-controlling interests) presented separately
- US GAAP (ASC 810): similar consolidation requirements but different threshold tests

## 8. Data Residency Requirements

### Privacy Law Comparison

| Region | Law | Key Data Residency Requirement | Penalties |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Brazil** | LGPD (Lei 13.709/2018) | No strict localization mandate, but international transfers require adequate protection or specific safeguards. ANPD (national authority) enforces. | Up to 2% of revenue in Brazil, capped at R$50M per infraction |
| **EU** | GDPR (Regulation 2016/679) | Data transfers outside EU require adequacy decision, SCCs, or BCRs. EU data should stay in EU unless adequate protections exist. Applies to anyone processing EU residents' data. | Up to 4% global annual turnover or €20M (whichever higher) |
| **UK** | UK GDPR + Data Protection Act 2018 | Post-Brexit, mirrors EU GDPR. International transfers require UK adequacy regulations or appropriate safeguards. | Up to £17.5M or 4% global turnover |
| **US** | No federal privacy law (state-level: CCPA/CPRA in CA, others) | No federal data localization. Some sector-specific rules (GLBA for financial data, HIPAA for health). California: CCPA applies to businesses serving CA residents. | CCPA: $2,500-$7,500 per violation |
| **Mexico** | LFPDPPP | Federal law, requires consent for data processing. No strict localization but requires disclosure of international transfers. | INAI can impose fines |
| **Colombia** | Law 1581 of 2012 | Requires registration with SIC (Superintendencia de Industria y Comercio). International transfers allowed with adequate protections. | SIC can impose fines |
| **Argentina** | PDPA (Law 25.326) | EU adequacy decision in place — Argentina recognized as providing adequate protection. | fines up to ARS 100,000 |

### Practical Architecture Implications
- **Brazil→EU**: LGPD and GDPR overlap. Need dual compliance. EU data must stay in EU or go to adequate jurisdictions. Use EU cloud regions (AWS eu-west, Azure West Europe).
- **Brazil→US**: No strict localization, but GLBA (Gramm-Leach-Bliley) applies to financial data. CCPA if serving California residents. SOC 2 compliance expected.
- **EU internal**: Single GDPR regime. Can process across EU/EEA freely. Lead supervisory authority model (one-stop shop).
- **UK**: Separate from EU post-Brexit. Needs own DPO, own supervisory authority relationship with ICO.

## Key Findings Summary

1. **Pix is the killer advantage**: Brazil's instant payment infrastructure is world-leading (200M users, R$3.4T/month). Any cashflow platform must deeply integrate Pix. The US government investigation into Pix (2025) highlights its global significance.

2. **LatAm-first is the right sequence**: Mexico and Colombia share IFRS, have VAT systems closer to Brazil, and have emerging instant payment systems. The US is a different regulatory universe.

3. **US expansion requires massive compliance investment**: 50+ state Money Transmitter Licenses, no unified sales tax, US GAAP (different from IFRS), no VAT. Consider US as a later-stage expansion or enter via acquisition/partnership.

4. **EU offers passport advantage**: One PSD2 license in Ireland or Netherlands grants access to 27 countries. But GDPR compliance is non-trivial and expensive.

5. **Tax system is the hardest localization layer**: Brazil's ICMS/IPI/ISS/PIS/COFINS complexity has no parallel. Each target country needs dedicated tax configuration. The platform must support both VAT and sales tax paradigms.

6. **IFRS is the accounting lingua franca**: Brazil, Mexico, Colombia, EU all use IFRS. The US (US GAAP) is the outlier. Design for IFRS first, add GAAP as a US-specific module.

7. **Data residency is architectural**: LGPD (Brazil), GDPR (EU/UK), CCPA (California) all require different data handling. The platform needs per-region data isolation capabilities.

8. **Correspondent banking is declining**: De-risking is reducing cross-border banking access. Fintech alternatives (Wise, d-local, stablecoins) are increasingly necessary for LatAm corridors.

9. **Multi-entity is mandatory**: Each country needs a local legal entity for licensing, tax, and regulatory compliance. Transfer pricing documentation is required from day one.

10. **Open Banking creates opportunity**: Brazil's Open Banking/Finance, EU's PSD2, UK's Open Banking mandate all require API-first architecture — which a modern cashflow platform should have anyway.
