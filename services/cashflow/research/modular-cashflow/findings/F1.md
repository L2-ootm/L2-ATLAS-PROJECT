# F1: Core Financial Modules

## Findings

### [1] General Ledger is the central system of record — every other module posts into it
- quote: "A general ledger is a bookkeeping ledger in which accounting data are posted from journals and aggregated from subledgers, such as accounts payable, accounts receivable, cash management, fixed assets, purchasing and projects."
- url: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_ledger
- source_type: secondary
- published: 2026-06-27
- confidence: high

### [2] General ledger is divided into at least 7 categories: assets, liabilities, owner's equity, revenue, expenses, gains and losses
- quote: "The general ledger is usually divided into at least seven main categories: assets, liabilities, owner's equity, revenue, expenses, gains and losses."
- url: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_ledger
- source_type: secondary
- published: 2026-06-27
- confidence: high

### [3] General Ledger feeds both the balance sheet and the income statement — it is the single source for all financial statements
- quote: "An organization's statement of financial position and the income statement are both derived from income and expense account categories in the general ledger."
- url: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_ledger
- source_type: secondary
- published: 2026-06-27
- confidence: high

### [4] Chart of Accounts (COA) is a list of financial accounts and reference numbers grouped into categories used for recording transactions in the general ledger
- quote: "A chart of accounts (COA) is a list of financial accounts and reference numbers, grouped into categories, such as assets, liabilities, equity, revenue and expenses, and used for recording transactions in the organization's general ledger."
- url: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chart_of_accounts
- source_type: secondary
- published: 2026-02-04
- confidence: high

### [5] A full IFRS/US GAAP-compliant COA requires 7 account classes: Assets, Liabilities, Equity, Revenue, Expenses, Other Income/Expenses, and Intercompany
- quote: "A chart of accounts compatible with IFRS and US GAAP includes balance sheet (assets, liabilities and equity) and the profit and loss (revenue, expenses, gains and losses) classifications. If used by a consolidated or combined entity, it also includes separate classifications for intercompany transactions and balances."
- url: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chart_of_accounts
- source_type: secondary
- published: 2026-02-04
- confidence: high

### [6] Accounts Payable is a form of credit — suppliers allow payment after goods/services are received, and the AP process involves three-way matching (invoice, packing slip, purchase order)
- quote: "AP is a form of credit that suppliers offer to their customers by allowing them to pay for a product or service after it has already been received... When the invoice is received by the purchaser, it is matched to the packing slip and purchase order, and if all is in order, the invoice is paid. This is referred to as the three-way match."
- url: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accounts_payable
- source_type: secondary
- published: 2026-07-03
- confidence: high

### [7] Accounts Receivable are legally enforceable claims for payment — they appear as current assets on the balance sheet and have two measurement methods (allowance method vs. direct write-off)
- quote: "Accounts receivable are legally enforceable claims for payment held by a business for goods supplied or services rendered that customers have ordered but not paid for... Companies have two methods available to them for measuring the net value of accounts receivable: the allowance method and the direct write-off method."
- url: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accounts_receivable
- source_type: secondary
- published: 2026-07-09
- confidence: high

### [8] Bank reconciliation compares entity books to bank statements — three causes of discrepancy: timing differences, bank-recorded-but-entity-unrecorded transactions, and errors
- quote: "In bookkeeping, bank reconciliation is the process by which the bank account balance in an entity's books of account is reconciled to the balance reported by the financial institution in the most recent bank statement. Any difference between the two figures needs to be examined and, if appropriate, rectified."
- url: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bank_reconciliation
- source_type: secondary
- published: 2026-04-18
- confidence: high

### [9] Double-entry bookkeeping is required by law for public companies under US GAAP and UK Companies Act — it maintains the accounting equation (Assets = Liabilities + Equity) through equal and opposite entries
- quote: "Double-entry accounting is the inherent, foundational system required to produce financial statements that comply with US Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP) and the reporting mandates of the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) for public companies. Section 396 of the UK Companies Act 2006 mandates these reports."
- url: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double-entry_bookkeeping
- source_type: secondary
- published: 2026-04-28
- confidence: high

### [10] A fiscal year is a one-year interval used for financial reporting — approximately 65% of US public companies use the calendar year (Jan-Dec), but many shift it for operational reasons
- quote: "The calendar year is used as the fiscal year by about 65% of publicly traded companies in the United States and for most large corporations in the United Kingdom. That is the case in many countries around the world with a few exceptions such as Australia, New Zealand, and Japan."
- url: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiscal_year
- source_type: secondary
- published: unknown
- confidence: high

### [11] Every jurisdiction requires taxes to be calculated on an annual basis corresponding to the fiscal year — the fiscal year system is a prerequisite for any tax calculation engine
- quote: "Taxation laws generally require accounting records to be maintained and taxes calculated on an annual basis, which usually corresponds to the fiscal year used for government purposes. The calculation of tax on an annual basis is especially relevant for direct taxes, such as income tax."
- url: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiscal_year
- source_type: secondary
- published: unknown
- confidence: high

### [12] COA structure is jurisdiction-dependent — France, Germany, Spain, and Sweden each define national GAAP charts of accounts, while the US and UK do not mandate a standard chart
- quote: "While some countries define standard national charts of accounts (for example France and Germany) others such as the United States and United Kingdom do not. In the European Union, most countries codify a national GAAP... the former often define a chart of accounts while the latter [IFRS] does not."
- url: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chart_of_accounts
- source_type: secondary
- published: 2026-02-04
- confidence: high
